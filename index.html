Context Women are pursuing formal education at increasing rates around the world, graduating from college in higher numbers than men in some countries. Women's labor force participation has increased in almost every country in the past three decades. 43% of educated women take a career break at some point to tend to their families, yet 90% say they would eventually like to return to work. Women are starting businesses at significant rates globally, but on average, women's businesses are smaller and are more likely to be informal or semiformal. An increasing proportion of women in developed economies are delaying marriage, but more are also cohabitating without marrying. Divorce rates have also been rising globally over the past few decades, with financial woes often cited as a top reason for divorce. Women of all income levels are less likely to own homes than men, and just 20% of the world's land is in women's names. Acquiring a primary home is a life goal for many women, and having investment property is also a goal for some.
Consideration Because of rising tertiary education costs, women hold a significant portion of student debt. When compounded with the gender pay gap that widens across women's lives, women can experience this as a double burden: higher debt and less capacity to pay it off. On average, women start their careers earning less than their male counterparts: in some cases due to unequal pay rates and in others due to the sectors they work in. Later in life, women often opt for part-time work or to take career breaks. These factors can make it harder for women to establish a strong financial basis. Women-owned/led businesses face a number of challenges in accessing capital sufficient to grow. Women tend to have less collateral than men, self-report lower business knowledge and take a different approach to risk. Marrying or committing to a partner often involves combining assets, which means women may also have to share a spouse's liabilities. Splitting up can hit women harder financially if they have lower earnings and assets. Women who cohabitate without marriage are at even greater risk of a financial hit, due to their lack of claim over household assets. The gender wage gap and other financial burdens women face can impact their capacity to afford purchasing or maintaining a home. There are also adverse cultural norms and marital legal regimes in some countries that limit women's rights: for example, their right to be on the title or to own more than 50% of a home.
Potential Solves Access to low-interest student loans, as well as information and advice on the process, can be valuable for women pursuing education. Repayment deferral based on employment status can help women transitioning to the workforce post-education. Leading up to this moment, a goal-based savings account may be useful. Information and tools around salary negotiation, financial planning and workforce re-entry could all prove highly useful for women in this life moment. Relevant business information; formalisation support; education on cash flow forecasting and financing; modifications to loan policies, including accepting non-traditional collateral; access to mentoring, coaching and networks; supplier diversity programs; and access to female-friendly incubators are a few ways FSPs can support women. Financial planning assistance and financial awareness trainings, accounts and tools that support with merging finances, loan repayment flexibility during separation, and access to legal advice may all be valuable. Accounts to help women save for a down-payment, flexible mortgages during pregnancy and maternity leave, home insurance with home technical assistance, and legal advice are all solutions that may deliver value to women in this life moment.

Phase 1 · Business Case

Internal Data Analysis

Sex-disaggregating Nedbank's client data across key datasets surfaces the women's opportunity and builds an evidence-based case for change.

The table below outlines the key indicators to extract and analyse from Nedbank's internal systems. Each metric should be cut by gender (where data exists) to reveal the gap between current performance and the women's segment opportunity. This disaggregated view is the foundation of a compelling internal business case.

Key Indicators & Why They Matter
Key Indicator Why it matters?
Active main-banked base Are women choosing Nedbank as their primary bank or holding secondary, low-value relationships? This is our core measure of wallet share.
# active credit accounts Breadth of women's participation across credit products (home loans, vehicle finance, cards, personal loans) and highlights underserved product categories.
Gross loans & advances (GLA) Quantifies the total credit exposure to women clients. A direct measure of lending inclusion and the revenue opportunity in growing this book.
Average credit exposure (loan balance) Signals whether women are accessing smaller or larger facilities, which may indicate risk aversion, affordability constraints, or systemic under-lending.
Net interest income (NII) Isolates the interest margin contribution from women's credit and deposit activity, revealing the profitability of the segment's core banking relationship.
# TP's & savings accounts (CASA) Measures the foundation of the banking relationship. Women with only TP's represent a clear cross-sell gap into savings, investment, and credit products.
Total retail deposit book Demonstrates the funding contribution women clients make to the bank's balance sheet.
Avg. deposit balance per client Highlights savings capacity and financial resilience. Lower balances may indicate income constraints or the need for tailored savings and investment products.
Total headline earnings (NIR + NII) Shows the full revenue picture: fee income plus interest income, enabling a true economic value assessment of the women's segment.
Advances-to-deposits ratio Exposes whether women are net depositors funding lending to other segments.
Credit loss ratio (CLR) Women globally demonstrate lower default rates (see global stats slide).
Cross-sell ratio (# of products/clients) A lower ratio relative to the mean / mens' segment indicates untapped revenue potential and a CVP that isn't meeting women's full financial needs.
Digital adoption rate Reveals channel preferences and accessibility barriers. High digital adoption supports a digitally-led CVP; low adoption may signal UX or trust gaps.
Avg. client tenure (yrs) Statistically women tend to be more loyal once main-banked. Longer tenure = higher lifetime value and lower acquisition cost.
Net promoter score Captures satisfaction and advocacy (provided we can evidence causation, i.e. "High NPS relative to (x), resulting in (y) behaviour or uptake).
Revenue per client The single most direct measure of segment monetisation. A gap between men and women points to either product gaps, pricing issues, or under-engagement.
Net client acquisition rate Tracks whether Nedbank is growing or losing share of the women's market.
